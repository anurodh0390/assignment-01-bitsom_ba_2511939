## Database Recommendation

For a healthcare startup building a core patient management system, I recommend **MySQL (RDBMS)**. Healthcare data is inherently structured and sensitive, requiring a "Safety First" approach. MySQL operates on the **ACID** model (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability), which guarantees that every transaction—such as updating a patient's medication or allergy list—is processed reliably and accurately. In a medical context, even a slight delay in data consistency (Eventual Consistency) could lead to life-threatening errors. According to the **CAP Theorem**, a patient management system must prioritize **Consistency and Availability** over Partition Tolerance to ensure that doctors always see the most accurate and up-to-date patient data.

However, my recommendation would shift toward a **Polyglot Persistence** strategy if the startup needed to add a **Fraud Detection Module**. Fraud detection requires the analysis of massive volumes of high-velocity, polymorphic data, such as login patterns, geographical inconsistencies, and behavioral metadata. These data points are often unstructured and change rapidly. 

In this scenario, **MongoDB** would be the superior choice for the fraud module due to its **BASE** model (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventual consistency) and horizontal scalability. MongoDB's flexible schema allows the system to ingest diverse data types without pre-defined tables. Therefore, the ideal architecture would use **MySQL** as the primary source of truth for clinical patient records to maintain strict integrity, while using **MongoDB** as a secondary analytical engine to power the real-time fraud detection algorithms. This hybrid approach leverages the strengths of both relational and NoSQL worlds.
